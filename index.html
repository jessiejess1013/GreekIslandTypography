Welcome to Samothrace!
A spiritual center from ancient times, home to the sanctuary of the great gods. Place of origin of the Nice of Samothrace, one of the most celebrated statues in the world, now prominently displayed in the Louvre. The island is also famous for waterfalls and ponds, one of which is nicknamed: The killer -- amateur climbers and swimmers best stay away!

Welcome to Rhodes!
Named after a rose, the largest island of the dodecanese was home to the Colossus of Rhodes, a huge statue of the sun god, the size of the statue of liberty in N.Y.! Also home to Valley of the butterflies where thousands butterflies of the species panxia gather, forming a breathtaking spectacle.

Welcome to Chios!
Famous in antiquity for its popular wine, exported all over the ancient world. Home to the Mastic trees whos resin is used to give confections a characteristic flavour to spirits and liqueurs, such as Chios's native drink Maticha, chewing gum, and a number of cakes, pastries, and desserts. Eugine Deacroiax painted the massacre at chios in 1824, which hangs in the Louvre. Oh yeah, and Homer was from there too.

Welcome to Zakynthos!
Prone to earthquakes, The flower of the Levant hosts the largest mediteranean nesting of the loggerhead turtle (Kareta-kareta). Planes are prohibited from taking off or landing during the night so as to not disturb them. The iconic Navagio beach starring a shipwrecked freighter from the 1980s features in many films and postcards. Zante is also the best place in the Ionian sea to get a decent curry.

Welcome to Kythnos!
Close to Athens, the low key lightning-shaped Kythnos the hidden island as its name suggests, has more than meets the eye. Thermal springs, superb honey, caves and fire-dancing, it only had two year-round inhabitants as late as the 1970s. A growing year round community settling there in the recent years has changed that. Apparently the place to be during carnival!

Welcome to Paros!
Birthplace - as his name implies - of the very famous greek folk singer Yannis Parios, the picturesque landscape and lifestyle of his native island inspired a collection of hit songs in the very popular nisiotika genre. Paros was naughty for siding with Persia in the Persian wars and punished by Athens for it, but made up for it later during the war of independence from the Ottomans, being home to one of the biggest leaders and heroines of the revolution, the formidable Manto Mavrogenous.

Welcome to Samos!
Worshipers of the Goddess Hera, clever builders of aqueducts, and producers of sweet wine, the Samiots had more than their fair share of ancient celebrities: Aesop, Epicuros, Aristarchus (the first to suggest a Heliocentric world), and even the mathematician Pythagoras, may have first sketched his famous theorem in the sand of a samiot beach.

Welcome to Euboea!
The second biggest island of Greece, connected to the mainland with two bridges, has a multitude of beautiful beaches, if you take the time to explore off the grid. Many of them glisten with layered flagstone rock, native to and ubiquitous on the island. In the strait of Evripos, splitting the city of Chalkida in the middle, you can observe the strange phenomenon of the crazy waters, the tide mysteriously changes direction every six hours. After the new moon, there is even a difference in level between the north and south Euboea gulfs...
